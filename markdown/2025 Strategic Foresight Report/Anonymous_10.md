Four challenges: 1. "Data as Capital" The forerunners of techno-feudalism GAFA-M are setting the standard for valuing data. Soon, the worth of data and the speed at which it is exchanged will surpass the gold standard and the analog era of capitalism. A failure to recognize the value of (personal) data from knowing someone's favorite beverage to the exact time they enter their car or get on a train, to the production capacity of an industry "competitor" is already generating massive profits for these digital empires. There is little public awareness of the consequences of this data mining, even as these companies construct "The Sims"-like digital twins of pretty much "everything". This is the tip of the iceberg as we fail to assess the impact of Google, Facebook, and TikTok in shaping collective behavior. Today, we see influencers subtly encroaching on personal space; tomorrow, we may witness hyper-targeted advertising that exploits biological signals such as a smartwatch detecting low blood sugar and triggering a perfectly timed beverage advertisement. From predicting to outright shaping the stock market or a political election, with growing room for speculation and other deviations... The collaboration of major corporations like in designing subliminal behavioral manipulation threatens autonomy across all levels (see Yuval Harari "Homo Deus" and other works). Worse, part of this data infrastructure used to extract and exploit information for profit is built with taxpayer money, offering no tangible health/wealth return to society (Citizens are "doubly" taxed for free profit of these companies). 2. Energy Consumption The exponential growth of data/digital comes with a corresponding surge in energy demand. Sustaining this data-driven world requires exponential energy resources, further straining existing infrastructure and accelerating environmental degradation (again, taxpayer money, no return) 3. "Carbon as Capital" failure & Adaptation: Two critical realizations will have to be translated into better policies a) EU Initiatives to "decarbonize" Europe are insufficient to meet the energy demands of a future highly technological society. b) While reducing carbon emissions remains the most accessible way to mitigate human impact on the biosphere, the relationship between emissions reduction and climate stabilization is not immediate or linear. Like a pot of simmering water, turning off the heat does not stop the boiling instantaneously. Reversing centuries of industrial impact within a single century is unrealistic. Instead, the focus might shift toward accelerating climate-resilient technologies and improving adaptive (techno/social) responses/strategies. 4. Mastering the Massive Migration Trends: In the short term, worsening living conditions driven by rising heat, water scarcity, and declining food production will force large-scale migration from regions surrounding the Mediterranean. The current strategy of postponing meaningful solutions will soon reach its breaking point. Resilience - EU Policy 1. Flexible: The European Union must adopt increasingly flexible policies to navigate these challenges faster and more effectively. 2. Scientific/technical: As the direct effects of climate change accumulate, and the indirect ones in the global south spill over, international collaboration may have to give way to more stringent "scientific" needs and policies. See success stories like CFCs, Covid-vaccine, etc. 3. AI-Driven: Artificial intelligence should play a role in shaping policy and governance. However, the root cause of today's crisis lies in political elites consistently siding with wealth owners for short-term gains. If European democracy & society do not become the genuine focus of decision-making potentially guided by AI then the 21st century risks becoming a technological "Middle Age". 4. Focused: resource scarcity may result in further pooling of policy targets, like developing a stable, scalable fusion process.
