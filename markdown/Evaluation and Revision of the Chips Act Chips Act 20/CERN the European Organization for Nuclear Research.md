CERN, the European Organization for Nuclear Research, operates the largest particle physics accelerator in the world, conducting leading research in fundamental physics in Europe. CERN is engaged in building some of the worlds largest and most complex scientific instruments to study the basic constituents of matter: the fundamental particles. Custom-designed microelectronic components, developed using advanced technologies, are essential elements of these sophisticated instruments. These microelectronics are tailored to meet the stringent operational requirements of CERNs unique particle-physics detectors. Their complexity arises from the need to operate reliably in extreme environments while delivering high precision and fast data processing. Achieving such performance relies on state-of-the-art deep-submicron semiconductor technologies, combined with advanced packaging solutions and 3D silicon integration techniques. These approaches enable highly integrated, radiation-tolerant, and efficient circuit architectures optimized for the demanding applications of particle physics experiments. The EU Chips Act aims to strengthen Europes semiconductor ecosystem, spanning research, design, manufacturing, and advanced packaging. For CERN, this framework could provide direct support for the development of custom microelectronic components required by its particle-physics detectors. Specifically: Access to advanced technologies: Chips Act initiatives, such as the ChipsJU pilot lines and design platforms, offer European researchers and institutes access to state-of-the-art deep-submicron processes and advanced packaging and 3D silicon integration technologies. Moreover, ChipsJU can enhance the access to state-of-the-art microelectronics EDA tools, enabling European researchers and engineers to efficiently design and optimise the complex custom components for net-generation particle physics instruments.
