As an academic, I can contribute to European microelectronics sovereignty mainly in two ways: educate the future workforce and research novel solutions before they are ready for industry. In a capital-intensive field like microelectronics, an academic cannot be successful without help, and the Chips Act 2 should address these issues. We need an extension of the current Europractice project. It needs enough funding to support modern technologies at affordable MPW prices, and it needs new contracts with the EDA industry to provide essential, tapeout-ready IP like clock generation, SERDES, and memory controllers for academic usage. Without this support, we will not be able to innovate in a relevant manner, and our graduates will have no practical experience with state-of-the-art technology. To put it short: without this help from the Chips Act 2, we will lose further in comparison to other world regions. Novel technologies, like, for example, neuromorphic computing or brain-machine interfaces, will rely heavily on modern silicon technologies, and Europe is still competitive in these areas. If this shall last, the Chips Act 2 will have to set aside relevant funds to enable prototyping access, including IP and software, for academia.
