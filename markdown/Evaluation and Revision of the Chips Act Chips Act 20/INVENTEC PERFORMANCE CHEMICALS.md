Europes ambition to restore its technological sovereignty in semiconductors requires addressing not only chip production itself, but the entire value chain that enables electronics production. Our company is involved in the development, manufacturing and supply of materials of soldering, sintering, cleaning, cooling and protecting for the assembly of semiconductor, and electronic circuit. Those materials are indispensable to front-end, back-end and electronics manufacturing as they are used across strategic industries such as aerospace, AI, defence, energy, medical, mobility and telecommunications. The first Chips Act marked a decisive step, yet it overlooked several strategic links: advanced chemical materials, interconnection, assembly, cleaning, cooling and coating essential processes without which the semiconductor and industrial reliability standards of end markets cannot be achieved. This value chain is today fragmented and heavily dependent on non-European suppliers, especially in chemicals and metallurgy. Securing European chip manufacturing while leaving these segments exposed would continue to weaken the resilience of the whole ecosystem. The shift toward wide-bandgap semiconductors reinforces this need. These new materials operate under harsher electrical and thermal conditions, requiring interconnection solutions with excellent conductivity, heat dissipation and high-temperature resistance. Strengthening European capabilities in these domains is therefore a prerequisite for competitiveness in next-generation power electronics. Cleaning and cooling processes are equally critical. Cleaning ensures surface integrity at every stage of chip fabrication, especially after etching, while cooling dissipates the extreme heat generated during patterning. Historically based on PFAS, these processes now face major regulatory scrutiny. Supporting European companies in developing PFAS-free, recyclable and low-impact alternatives would significantly reduce the environmental footprint of semiconductor production while positioning Europe ahead of tightening global regulations. To achieve these goals, the Chips Act 2 should actively support R&D, industrial deployment and qualification of advanced materials and processes from low-TRL research to pilot lines and full-scale manufacturing. This includes promoting stronger collaboration between local industries, RTOs, public research, start-ups, SMEs and mid-caps, particularly in design, advanced chemistry, assembly and thermal management. Europe already hosts high-performing players in these areas, but they remain insufficiently connected and often overlooked compared to large manufacturers. Technological sovereignty cannot be achieved through isolated flagship fabs alone. It requires an integrated and sustainable ecosystem in which each link materials, equipment, design, interconnection, assembly, cleaning and cooling is recognized, supported and financed. Extending the Chips Act 2 to these segments would send a strong signal: Europe intends to invest where reliability, safety, sustainability and competitiveness truly begin
