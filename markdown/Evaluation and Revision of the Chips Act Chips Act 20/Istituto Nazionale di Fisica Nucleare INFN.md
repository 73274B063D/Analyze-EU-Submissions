The European Chips Act is a highly valuable initiative, and its continuation, expansion, and reinforcement are indispensable. The structural gaps that Europe faces across the semiconductor value chain cannot be closed within only a few years; addressing them requires sustained, long-term programs that ensure continuity, predictability, and stability. It is very important that these programs remain inclusive in scope, engaging all actors capable of contributing to the shared objective of strengthening the European Unions competitiveness in the strategically vital domain of microelectronics. As a research and technology organization developing complex instrumentation for fundamental science and related applications, we would like to highlight two specific areas where further emphasis would generate significant impact. The first concerns the development of integrated electronics and systems designed to operate in extreme environmentsspecifically, temperatures well outside the conventional 40 째C to +125 째C range and/or high-radiation conditions. Such specialized circuits are essential for state-of-the-art scientific instrumentation, including accelerator-based experiments, neutrino and dark-matter detectors, and large astroparticle observatories, all of which depend on custom high-performance integrated systems capable of maintaining stability and precision under challenging conditions. Similar requirements arise in several other strategic domains: emerging fields such as quantum technologies and fusion-reactor diagnostics; rapidly expanding sectors such as space exploration and Earth-observation systems; and consolidated industries, including automotive. Electronics for extreme environments therefore represent an area in which the EU still holds a significant opportunity to achieve international leadership within a relatively short timeframe. However, the reliable design of CMOS-based integrated circuits for such environments is currently hindered by several technical and structural barriers. These include the limited availability of foundry models and digital libraries characterized for extreme temperatures or high-radiation operation, as well as the shortage of more complex reusable IP blockssuch as high-resolution data converters, phase-locked loops, timing circuits, and digital signal-processing coresspecifically adapted to harsh-environment constraints. Addressing these gaps would substantially accelerate innovation across scientific, industrial, and emerging markets and would constitute the naturaland necessarycomplement to the important efforts already underway in wide-bandgap semiconductor technologies. The second key point is the persistent shortage of skilled professionals across the semiconductor ecosystem, and particularly in integrated-circuit design. Efforts to attract, educate, and retain talent must be further intensified, with special attention to mechanisms that effectively bridge the gap between academic training and industrial practice. Such mechanisms are especially valuable for small and medium-sized enterprises, which often lack the resources and risk-tolerance needed to train new personnel independently. In this context, organizations capable of providing practical, realistic use casesand that are inherently more resilient to the risks associated with involving in-training personnel in advanced projectscan play a pivotal role. By offering hands-on experience across full design cycles, these organizations can significantly accelerate workforce development, strengthen Europes semiconductor capabilities, and ensure that the objectives of the European Chips Act translate into long-term, sustainable impact.
