feedbackTextUserLanguage: I cannot get out of the feeling that Chips Act 1 has become
  a political and academic topic with minimal real outcomes. Despite extensive legislation,
  the EU’s share of chip production by 2025 has not even increased by 1 % compared
  to the rest of the world. Backwardness tends to widen. At the same time, support
  for chip production has been discussed for a relatively long time. According to
  the Call for Evidence..., the Chips Act, in force since September 2023, has already
  led to over EUR 80 billion of announced investment in production capacity. This
  is a relatively large amount, but this has not yet been reflected in the catching-up
  of Asia or at least the US in this area. Moreover, some of the projects seem to
  have been delayed or even cancelled. Throughout the legislative and administrative
  chain, there is a lack of concrete action and targets that are quantifiable and
  measurable at different points in time. Similarly, there is no definition of responsibility
  and consequential consequences for non-compliance with the indicators. However,
  what is no less important is the fact that the chips themselves are unusable without
  being installed on printed circuit boards (Printed Circuit Boards PCBs). Chips will
  not fly in the air and their inputs and outputs will not be connected by light conductors.
  They shall be soldered to a printed circuit board (PCB) where copper conductors
  ensure that the chips are powered and the outlets (chip contacts) are connected
  to other electronic components to form a single functional electronic unit. Without
  quality printed circuits, it is unnecessary to deal with chips themselves. Similarly,
  the existing Chip Act adopted in the US is followed by a legislative standard known
  as the PCBs Act, which was submitted to Congress by Blake Moore for approval shortly
  after the Chip Act was approved. In this way, the Americans realised both the strategic
  importance of PCBs and the high dependence on the supply of PCBs from China. It
  is the production of PCBs in a catastrophic state throughout the EU. Still in 2000,
  there were more than 610 PCB producers in countries that make up the EU today, while
  in 2025 this number fell to 188 producers. And this is decreasing every year. Therefore,
  if we do not include in Chip Act 2 the topic of support for PCBs production, our
  dependence on China for the production of electronics as such will only increase.
  This applies both to consumer electronics and to electronics intended for strategic
  military, aerospace, automotive and, for example, the medical industry. For example,
  the abolition of import duties on basic materials (possibly the introduction of
  import duties on PCBs imported from the Far East) is very timely. It is also strategically
  important to legislate that defence and other strategically important products are
  produced exclusively with European printed circuit boards (PCBs). Or do we want
  to continue to provide our patented solutions to third countries and Know How by
  providing them with complete technical documentation for the production, installation
  and even recovery of PCBs? The Chips Act 2 cannot be a mere political declaration,
  but must be a very serious topic for expert debate, aiming at outputs that can be
  applied in practice in the short term so that we are at least left behind, but above
  all, so that we can achieve the greatest possible autonomy and thus independence
  from the supply of any electronic components from Asia, at least in defined areas.
  Politicians as well as the Commission should therefore contact e.g. IPC experts
  (by way of example, they should already have an office directly in Brussels for
  this purpose) or, for example, organisations such as ZVEI e.V., the German Electrical
  and Electronic Manufacturers’ Association, which also has an office in Brussels.
